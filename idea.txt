1. Philosophy & How to Use This
This is a complete system — physical training, conditioning, nutrition, recovery, and mental
toughness — built to develop the kind of strength, endurance, and grit that Alan Ritchson’s
character “81” embodies in War Machine (2026). It blends bodybuilding-style hypertrophy
work (for size and visual strength) with Army Ranger conditioning (for real-world endurance
and resilience).

Run it for 8–12 weeks, then reassess. Deload every 5–6 weeks.

The three pillars, ranked by actual importance:

1. Consistency — showing up 6 days a week for months

2. Recovery — sleep, food, stress management

3. Programming — the actual workouts

Most people obsess over #3 and neglect #1 and #2. Don’t.

THURSDAY — ZONE 2 ENDURANCE
This is the day most people skip. Don’t.

Protocol: 45–60 min continuous, easy pace. You can nose-breathe the whole time OR hold
a full conversation without gasping. Target HR: roughly 180 minus your age.

If you finish feeling “I could do that again” — that’s the right effort. If you’re cooked, you
went too hard.

Options (rotate to prevent boredom):


  Option                                  Notes

  Easy jog outdoors                       Default. Conversation pace.

  Incline treadmill walk                  10–15% grade, 3.0–3.5 mph. Easier on joints.

  Unloaded hike                           Hills are fine; just keep effort easy.

  Bike (road or stationary)               Lowest joint impact. Great option.

  Swim (easy laps)                        Full recovery from impact sports.

  Rowing (steady state)                   Full-body, low impact.

  Elliptical                              Boring but effective.

  Ruck (light)                            15–20 lbs only, not the heavy Saturday load.


No lifting today. Recovery is the point.

<a name="gtg"></a>


5. Daily Grease-the-Groove Pull-ups
Goal: accumulate quality reps without fatigue.

Rules:

   Never to failure. If your max is 10, do 3–5 reps per set.
   5–8 sets spread across the day — morning, before meals, end of day.

   Total: 20–40 reps daily.

   Skip on Friday (pull day).

   Take Sunday off.

If you don’t have a pull-up bar (alternatives):

   Inverted rows on a sturdy table

   Bodyweight rows on a low bar

   Towel rows (towel over door)

   Band pull-aparts (50/day)

   Scapular pulls on any bar

Within 8 weeks, expect your max pull-up count to jump 30–50%.




<a name="scaling"></a>


6. Scaling by Experience Level

Beginner (new to lifting or conditioning, < 6 months consistent training)
   Only 4 training days for first 4 weeks: Mon legs, Wed push, Fri pull, Sat light ruck (3
   miles, 20 lbs)

   Main lifts at 3 sets instead of 4

   Thursday zone 2: 30 min instead of 45–60

   Skip intervals until Week 5

   Saturday circuit: half volume (25 push-ups, 12 pull-ups, 50 squats, 25 sit-ups)

   Build for 4 weeks before using the full template


Intermediate (6 months – 2 years consistent training)
   Follow the program as written.

   Rucks: 6–8 miles at 35 lbs on Saturday.
Advanced (2+ years consistent, solid base)
   Saturday ruck: 12 miles with 45–50 lbs (actual Ranger School standard)

   Add second zone 2 session Sunday morning (30 min easy)

   Thursday: extend zone 2 to 75–90 min every other week

   Add weighted vest to daily grease-the-groove pull-ups

   Consider one extra movement per main lift (5×5 strength block before hypertrophy)

<a name="recovery"></a>


8. Recovery & Sleep
Sleep 7–9 hours. The actual cheat code. 6 hours = no program works.

Other priorities:

   Sunday full rest, non-negotiable

   Deload every 5–6 weeks (40% less weight, same movements, one week)

   Sore 3+ days? Pull back 10%

   Resting HR trending up 5+ bpm for a week? You’re overreaching — extra rest day

   10 min mobility after every workout (not optional — it’s injury insurance)

Recovery signals to track weekly:


  Signal                     Good                        Red flag

  Resting HR (morning)       Stable or trending down     Up 5+ bpm for a week

  Sleep quality              7–9 hrs, feel rested        <6 hrs or waking frequently

  Mood                       Steady, motivated           Irritable, dreading workouts

  Appetite                   Normal or elevated          Suppressed for several days

  Performance                Progressing or steady       Weights dropping for 2+ sessions

  Joint pain                 Mild or absent              Sharp or persistent


Multiple red flags = extra rest day or early deload.




<a name="grit"></a>
9. The Grit Protocol
Grit is trained the same way as strength: small, specific, repeatable reps.


Daily (every day, non-negotiable)
Morning (5 min):

   Write ONE specific promise for the day (“I will do X at Y time”)

   State your identity sentence (“I am someone who ____”)

Throughout the day:

   Cold finish on every shower (last 60 sec on cold)

   One chosen hard thing (see Section 10)

   Self-talk check: swap “I can’t” for operational language (“next rep,” “one more”)

Evening (3 min):

   Did I keep my promise? (Y/N)

   What was my hard thing today?

   Where did I break — and what triggered it?


Weekly
Sunday review (15 min):

1. Promise rate: how many days did I keep my daily promise? (Target: 6/7)

2. Pattern: what did broken promises have in common?

3. Win of the week: one specific moment I pushed through when I didn’t want to

4. Next week’s one focus: one thing I’ll do differently

Sunday planning:

   Write the week’s promises in advance

   Pre-commit to Saturday’s Grit Day

   Identify the day most likely to break; make a specific plan for it


Every 4 weeks
   Re-read all four weekly reviews

   What’s changed? What hasn’t?

   Update identity statement if needed

   Add one new hard thing to the menu




<a name="hardthing"></a>


10. The Hard Thing Menu
Rotate. Don’t do the same one every day.


Physical (10 min or less)
   Cold shower, 2+ minutes

   Set to true failure (any lift, safely)

   100 burpees for time

   Walk outside in bad weather, 20 min, no phone

   Last mile of a run at threshold pace

   Fast from breakfast to lunch (skip one meal intentionally)

   5 min plank (broken as needed)

   Max push-ups in 2 min


Mental
   20 min focused work, phone in another room

   Read 10 pages of something hard (not fiction, not your field)

   Sit still for 10 min doing nothing — no phone, no music

   Write for 10 min without editing

   Do a task you’ve been putting off for 2+ weeks

   Learn something you’re bad at for 20 min


Social / emotional
   Have the conversation you’ve been avoiding

   Send the message you’ve been drafting

   Ask for something you’re afraid to ask for

   Admit you were wrong about something, out loud, to someone

   Give a sincere compliment to someone you don’t know well

   Sit with an uncomfortable emotion for 10 min without distracting

The rule: It has to be chosen and uncomfortable. If it’s easy today, level it up tomorrow.

11. Self-Talk Scripts
Memorize these. Use them under load.


  Situation                                     Script

  Rep count says stop                           “One more. Just one. Then decide.”

  Workout feels impossible before starting      “I don’t have to finish it. I just have to start it.”

  Mile 6 of 10 with loaded pack                 “Next pole. Just the next pole.”

  Motivation is gone                            “Motivation isn’t coming. Go anyway.”

  You’ve broken a promise                       “That was one. The next one is separate.”

  In the middle of a hard set                   “This is the rep. This one. Right now.”

  Want to skip the workout                      “The schedule is the training. Go.”

  Zone 2 feels boring                           “Boring is the work. Do the work.”

13. Return Protocol (When You Fail)
You will fail. Plan for it. The rule: Never miss twice.


  What
                        What to do
  happened

  Missed a              Do the next one as scheduled. Don’t “make it up.” Don’t restart. Just don’t
  workout               miss the next.

  Broke your
                        Note it in the log. Make a new promise for tomorrow. Keep it.
  promise

  Bad week              The Sunday review is more important, not less. Don’t skip it.

  Off 1 week            Resume at current weights. Expect a rough first session.

  Off 2–3 weeks         Restart at 80% of last weights. Rebuild for 1 week.
  Off 4+ weeks           Restart at 70%. Rebuild for 2 weeks. Don’t ego-lift back.

  Injured                See a physio. Work around it. Never “train through” sharp pain.

  Sick                   Rest until 24h symptom-free. Resume at 80% for 2–3 days.


The spiral is the enemy, not the miss. One skipped day is noise. Two is a pattern. Three is
a new habit. Kill it at one.

<a name="onboarding"></a>


17. First 2-Week Onboarding Checklist

Week 1
   Buy rucksack, pull-up bar, HR monitor

   Baseline test day: 1-mile run time, max push-ups in 2 min, max pull-ups, ruck pace at 25
   lbs for 2 miles

   Weigh yourself. Measure waist at navel. Photo in same lighting.

   Write your identity sentence. One. Same one for at least 30 days.

   Hit all 6 training days (or 4 if beginner)

   Start grease-the-groove pull-ups daily

   Log every workout — weights, reps, how it felt

   Start daily log (morning promise, evening 3 questions)


Week 2
   Add weight where you hit top of rep range cleanly

   Sleep 7+ hours every night

   Hit protein target 6 of 7 days

   First real zone 2 session with HR monitor

   First Sunday review — honest one

   Buy first recommended book. Start reading.

   Identify the day most likely to break next week. Plan for it.


Ongoing
   Reassess baselines every 4 weeks
   Deload every 5–6 weeks

   Monthly: re-read all 4 weekly reviews

   If numbers aren’t moving after 4 weeks, audit sleep and food first — not the program




The Uncomfortable Truth
None of this is secret. None of it is complicated.

People who seem to have inhuman willpower aren’t using different techniques — they’ve just
been doing these boring things consistently for years.

There’s no hack. There’s no shortcut. There’s just today’s rep. Do it. Then do tomorrow’s.

The first one is the hardest.
